Wiki is a website, a publication on the internet, a database, an online community that can be managed, modified, and contributed to by its users and audience directly through a web browser [1], [2], [3]. Wikis are community-driven projects, one of the most popular ways of reading, writing, and sharing information online [3]. They are powered by wiki software, its engine [2], [3]. That is a content management system, and it differs from the rest of web-based systems as the content is created without an owner or a leader - every user is able to add, edit or remove content. Wiki was envisioned essentially as a software development tool [4]. It is important to note that Wiki and Wikipedia are not the same [2]! Wikipedia is an online encyclopedia [2], [5]. It is the most popular and the largest wiki-based website with the greatest cultural impact as it is one of the most popular websites on the internet in general. Wikipedia is not a single wiki but rather a collection of hundreds of wikis.


Back in 1994, the creation of Wikis began by the American computer programmer Ward Cunningham as he wanted to create a new collaborative technology for organizing information on websites [1], [6]. Cunningham called this new software “WikiWikiWeb”, using the Hawaiian term “wiki” meaning “quick”. His idea for the name of the software correlates with how he originally described Wiki as “the simplest online database that could possibly work" [2]. He liked the name as it had an attractive alliteration and the same abbreviation as the World Wide Web – WWW [1], [4].


There are hundreds of thousands of Wikis in use today [2]. Some Wikis are free, while some are proprietary. Some are public while some are private. Some can even permit control over different levels of access such as editing rights. Many other rules can be imposed depending on what the Wiki will be used for. Wikis can be used for professional or personal reasons, they can function not only to find or contribute information, but as knowledge management resources, note-taking tools, community websites, and intranets [3], [2]. As much as Wikis can be an amazing tool, they are often not reliable for gathering information as they are open-source software [2]. Unfortunately, the statements in Wikipedia, for example, are not always correct [4]. The Wikipedia community has no shared assumptions on defining and verifying truth. The basis of many critics' complaints with Wikipedia lies in the fact that they view the project against the ideal of a singular, verifiable truth, while Wikipedia envisions itself as a project wide enough to host competing truths.
